Learn about the importance of Networking!

Networking keeps your goals visible to the people who can champion your work. A quick conversation can be the moment someone thinks of you when an opportunity opens, helping you “get your foot in the door” where you otherwise wouldn’t have.

  • Learn about internships and full-time roles before they’re widely posted.
  • Stay on the radar of mentors who can advocate for you with a referral or warm introduction.
  • Gain real-world context about industries, teams, and skills so you feel confident walking into interviews.

Networking is the process of making connections and building relationships. When you follow up thoughtfully, those relationships can grow into collaborations, mentoring relationships, or friendships that support your personal and professional goals.

Modern networking happens everywhere—LinkedIn DMs, Slack communities, conferences, office hours, and even quick coffee chats. Focus on curiosity and shared interests first; the opportunities follow naturally.

Networking can feel intimidating at first, so start with communities that already want to see you succeed.

Your professors

  • Use office hours to build genuine relationships and get personalized advice.
  • When you need a letter of recommendation, they’ll already understand your interests and strengths.

LinkedIn

  • Search for University of Michigan alumni at your dream companies and study their career paths.
  • Reach out with thoughtful questions about their experiences and advice on breaking into similar roles.

Family and friends

  • Let people close to you know what you’re exploring—they may be one introduction away from someone working in your field.

UMSI events

  • Career fairs, employer info sessions, and mentorship programs give you direct access to recruiters and alumni.
  • Bring updated resumes, ask questions, and collect contact information so you can follow up.

UMSI alumni

  • Tap LinkedIn groups and the UMSI Alumni Association to find people who have been in your shoes and can share actionable guidance.

Informational interviews (often called “coffee chats”) are the fastest way to understand a role, team culture, and hiring timelines. Whether you meet virtually or at a café, keep the conversation light, curious, and respectful of time.

Not everyone will reply—and that’s okay. Treat each outreach as a learning experience and organize your “leads” so you can follow up.

Warm leads

A warm lead is an alumni, recruiter, or stakeholder who shares a connection with you and can introduce you to hiring managers or referrals for specific opportunities.

  • Use advanced LinkedIn searches or UMSI communities to surface alumni with shared experiences.
  • Ask professors or mentors if they can facilitate an introduction via email or LinkedIn.

Cold leads

A cold lead is someone you don’t yet know but admire from afar. They may be outside the U-M network, so personalization matters.

  • Reference their recent projects or something specific from their profile to show sincere interest before asking for time.

Here’s an outreach template you can adapt:

Hi [Name],
I’m a [year/degree] student focusing on [area]. I’ve been following your work at [Company/Team] and I’m especially interested in [specific thing they do].
If you’re open to it, could I have 15–20 minutes of your time next week for an informational chat about how you got into [role] and which skills matter most? I’m free [2–3 time windows] but happy to work around your schedule.
Thanks so much either way, and if there’s someone you recommend I speak with, I’d appreciate the referral.
Best,
[Your Name] · [Program] · [LinkedIn/Portfolio link]

Tip: AI tools can help you draft messages quickly, but always edit them to sound like you.

Remember, networking is about building relationships, not just asking for favors. Be genuine, be curious, and be respectful of people's time.
